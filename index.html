Harley Wood School of Astronomy 2026

Bridging Observation and Theory in the Age of Big Data Astronomy

In conjunction with the Annual Scientific Meeting, the ASA sponsors the Harley Wood School of Astronomy for postgraduate students each year. The School is named in memory of Dr Harley Wood, first President of the ASA.

About the school

The School will feature 2 days of lectures and hands-on sessions, with the remaining time dedicated to networking activities. The registration fee includes 3 nights of acommodation, transport to and from the venue, and all meals. This year, we are focusing on the topic of linking observations and simulations, with particular emphasis on the role of high-performance computing, big data, and machine learning in this process.

Connecting Observations and Simulations

Learn how to use both astronomical observations and computational simulations together to enhance your research, with a focus on practical strategies for integrating both in your science cases.

Modern Methods

The school will cover advanced techniques used in today’s research, including high-performance computing, big data analysis, and machine learning.

Future Facilities and Surveys

We will explore upcoming astronomical facilities and surveys, and learn how to consider future developments when planning your research.

We are living through a revolution in Milky Way observational astronomy. Gaia and a wave of ground-based spectroscopic surveys now provide element abundances and stellar kinematics for millions of stars. We now have data that can test galaxy formation at a scale and resolution long anticipated. But more data does not automatically mean more understanding, and reaching for more complex models and advanced tools is not always the right first response. I will argue that the most productive path forward is to uncover the simplicity in the data: to build models that are generative, physically interpretable, and no more complex than the data demands. I will walk through several results from data-driven astronomy that emerged not from complexity, but from restraint; asking careful questions, one at a time. The lesson, I think, is a general one for physical science in the big data era.

Radio astronomy opens a unique window onto the Universe, tracing everything from magnetised plasmas and cosmic dawn to the large-scale structure of galaxies. This lecture will provide a concise introduction to the foundations of radio astronomy, covering key emission processes, observational techniques, and the capabilities of modern and next-generation facilities. We will explore how radio observations confront and inform theoretical models, highlighting case studies in radio transient science where data and theory meet to reveal new physics. In the era of big data astronomy, radio astronomy sits at the forefront: instruments like the Square Kilometre Array pathfinders already produce petabyte-scale datasets, demanding novel approaches to data processing, simulation, and interpretation. You will find out why radio astronomy is intrinsically a data-intensive science with a hands-on example. We will discuss the computational challenges radio observations present; how emerging tools and techniques are reshaping the field and what radio astronomy will look like in the future.

I will tell the origin story of the 4MOST Hemisphere Survey (4HS) project, which will measure 4+ million galaxy spectra and redshifts starting in just a few weeks. I will try to spin out some stories to show what goes into getting a large project off the ground, the structures and tools that we are building to engage and support the development of ECRs through our team, and underline some of the lessons and skills that have made a difference. Two major themes will be the value of laboratories versus experiments, and how to make your mark within a large international collaboration.
2:30 PM - 3:30 PM

Modern astrophysical data often come from complex instruments and simulations where the likelihood is difficult or impossible to write down explicitly. In this hands-on Python workshop, we will explore the fundamentals of simulation-based inference (SBI): including Bayesian inference with forward models, prior predictive simulations, neural posterior estimation, calibration, and posterior predictive checking. We will work through astrophysical examples such as recovering gravitational-wave-like signals in noisy time-series data and discuss extensions to sparse VLBI imaging, highlighting the roles of simulator accuracy and model mismatch. Please bring your laptop and visit the GitHub repo for setup instructions.

Lecturers & mentors

The 2026 lecturer list will be announced closer to the school. Stay tuned!

Mark Krumholz

Research School of Astronomy and Astrophysics, ANU

Mark Krumholz completed his PhD at University of California, Berkeley in 2005, and then held a Hubble Fellowship at Princeton from 2005-2008. He joined the faculty at the University of California, Santa Cruz in 2008, serving as assistant and then associate professor, before moving The Australian National University as a professor in 2015, where he has remained since. His research interests are in theory of star formation and the interstellar medium, and numerical methods for large-scale simulations.

Melissa Ness

Research School of Astronomy and Astrophysics, ANU

Melissa Ness is an electrical engineer who moved "late" in life to astronomy research and has since had the opportunity to work at the Max Planck Institute of Astronomy in Heidelberg, at Columbia University and the Center for Computational Astrophysics in New York, and now The Australian National University in Canberra. She uses large survey data to reverse-engineer the formation history of the Milky Way Galaxy, and is particularly interested in developing data-driven machine learning approaches to extract information from stellar spectra.

Connor Bottrell

International Centre for Radio Astronomy Research at University of Western Australia

Connor is a Forrest Fellow at the International Centre for Radio Astronomy Research at UWA. He completed his PhD in Canada at the University of Victoria. From 2020-2023 he was a Kavli Fellow at the University of Tokyo's Institute for the Physics and Mathematics of the Universe (IPMU). His research focuses on applying AI to large data sets from modern telescopes and galaxy simulations to test models for baryonic and dark matter physics.

Ned Taylor

Centre for Astrophysics and Supercomputing at Swinburne University of Technology

Prof. Taylor is an extragalactic astronomer and sometimes cosmologist who maps galaxy demographics and the galaxy–halo connection in the relatively nearby Universe. He co-leads (with Michelle Cluver) the 4MOST Hemisphere Survey; a project to obtain spectra and redshifts for approx. 4 million galaxies across the full southern hemisphere to obtain the best possible description of galaxies at z < 0.15. His other main research activity is pioneering novel experimental designs to detect and measure the dark matter halos that surround galaxies via the effect of gravitational lensing.

Yuxiang Qin

Research School of Astronomy and Astrophysics, ANU

Dr Yuxiang Qin is currently an ARC DECRA Fellow at the Research School of Astronomy & Astrophysics, Australian National University. His research interests are in high-redshift galaxy formation, reionization, 21-cm cosmology, and their synergies with fundamental dark matter physics. Dr Qin integrates numerical simulations, Bayesian inference frameworks, and machine learning techniques to interpret high-redshift observations and evaluate the scientific potential of future instruments such as the Square Kilometre Array. He was awarded a PhD in astrophysics by the University of Melbourne in 2018.

George Heald

CSIRO

George Heald is the Lead Commissioning Scientist for the SKA-Low telescope, which is currently under construction in Western Australia. He received his PhD in 2006 at the University of New Mexico. He worked for a decade at ASTRON in the Netherlands, then moved to Australia in 2016 to work at CSIRO, eventually leading the ATNF Science program. He moved to the SKA Observatory in 2024. His research interests include neutral hydrogen in galaxies, cosmic magnetism, and “making radio telescopes work”.

Rajika Kuruwita

Research School of Astronomy and Astrophysics, ANU

Dr. Rajika Kuruwita returned to the Australian National University as a Postdoc, 7 years after she got her PhD there in 2019. During her time away she was an EU Interactions Fellow at the Niels Bohr Institute at the University of Copenhagen, and then she was the first HITS Independent Postdoc at the Heidelberg Institute for Theoretical Studies. Her work uses 3D Magnetohydrodynamical simulations to model the formation of binary and multiple star systems from cluster scales down to circumstellar discs. She likes to look at how early interactions between stars could help, or hinder planet formation.

Iris de Ruiter

University of Sydney

Dr. Iris de Ruiter is a postdoctoral researcher with OzGrav at the University of Sydney. She works on finding and making sense of radio transients, using large survey data and follow‑up observations with telescopes such as ASKAP, ATCA, and MeerKAT. She is particularly interested in long‑period radio transients and the quirky radio behaviour of white dwarf binaries. Iris earned her PhD cum laude from the University of Amsterdam, where she led systematic searches for low‑frequency radio transients using LOFAR.
